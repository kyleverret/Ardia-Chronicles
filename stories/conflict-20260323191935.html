Grand Army Field Medics Confirm Wounded Shadow Legion Soldiers Carry Grand Council Military Tattoos

Captured fighters bear unit markings from disbanded 7th Eastern Regiment. Veterans' records show soldiers officially listed as killed in action three years ago.

DA
By Dorian Ashveil War & Conflict Reporter
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

IRONKEEP FORWARD STATION — I have spent nine years documenting what happens when soldiers meet in battle. I thought I understood the boundaries of this war. I was wrong. Three field medics at Forward Station Blackthorn have independently confirmed to me that wounded Shadow Legion fighters captured during last week's skirmish at Ashenmoor Pass bear regimental tattoos matching the Grand Army's 7th Eastern Regiment — a unit officially disbanded after suffering total losses during the Blightwood incursion of Year 844 AP.

The tattoos are not forgeries. I examined two of the captured soldiers myself, with permission from Station Commander Aldric Vance. The ink patterns include the 7th Eastern's distinctive crossed-pike insignia and individual service numbers etched in the old Northern script. When I provided those service numbers to a contact in the Grand Army's Records Division, she went silent for six hours. When she returned my runescript, she said only: "Those men are listed as killed at Thornwall. Full military honors. Families notified."

The captured soldiers cannot explain their presence. They speak haltingly, in fragments, and display the same thousand-yard emptiness I witnessed in the prisoners who spoke identical words across Ironkeep's isolated cells last week. Field Medic Sergeant Corra Ashby — a woman I have trusted with my life on two previous embeds — told me their wounds heal at twice normal speed, but their eyes never focus on anything in front of them. "Whatever these men are now," she said, "they haven't been soldiers for a long time."

I brought this evidence to Commander Brin Ashfall of the 1st Praxis Legion, who has been my most reliable source for information the public deserves to know. She reviewed my documentation for twelve minutes without speaking. Then she said: "There are names I recognize here. Men I served with. Men whose memorial stones I helped place." She has authorized me to report that she is formally requesting a Grand Council inquiry into the 7th Eastern's casualty records.

The implications are not subtle. If Shadow Legion ranks include soldiers the Grand Army officially declared dead — soldiers whose families received death benefits and letters of condolence — then either our military's records are catastrophically wrong, or someone has been feeding our own people to the enemy we claim to be fighting. I have submitted formal inquiries to Lord Commander Severin Drath's office and the Grand Army's Personnel Command. Neither has responded. The men in those medical tents cannot tell me how they came to wear enemy colors. But the tattoos on their arms tell a story our commanders seem unwilling to hear.
