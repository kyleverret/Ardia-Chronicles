Grand Army Field Surgeons Discover Shadow Legion Dead Carrying Active-Duty Council Military Identification

Recovered documents show soldiers killed fighting for the Shadow Legion were still listed as serving in the Grand Army's 7th Eastern Regiment.

DA
By Dorian Ashveil War & Conflict Reporter
28 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

IRONKEEP EASTERN APPROACH — I held the identification papers myself. Three sets, recovered from three bodies wearing Shadow Legion black, each bearing the unmistakable seal of the Grand Army's personnel office and service dates that should have made these men my enemies' enemies. Instead, according to every official record that exists, Corporal Aldric Venn, Private First Class Mira Thornwell, and Sergeant Bram Coldwell died two months ago in a supply convoy ambush near Ashenmoor Pass—the same ambush I covered for this paper in March.

Field Surgeon Captain Elise Greymark showed me the documents in her medical tent last night, her hands steady in the way that only comes from years of cutting into the dying. 'The bodies came in with the morning's dead,' she said. 'Standard procedure—we catalog everything before burial. When I saw the papers, I thought someone had made a mistake. Then I checked the regimental tattoos.' The tattoos matched. The service numbers matched. The faces, she told me, matched the memorial portraits the 7th Eastern Regiment had commissioned after the Ashenmoor ambush to honor their fallen.

I reached Commander Brin Ashfall of the 1st Praxis Legion through secure channels this morning. She confirmed that three other field hospitals along the Shadow Frontier have reported similar discoveries in the past week—Grand Army soldiers, officially listed as killed in action, turning up dead again wearing enemy colors. 'We're not talking about deserters,' Ashfall said, her voice carrying the particular flatness I've learned to recognize from officers delivering news they wish they didn't have. 'Deserters don't keep their identification. Deserters don't stay on active-duty rosters months after they've supposedly died. Someone is moving bodies—or something worse.'

The Grand Army's Northern Command has not responded to my requests for comment. A spokesman for the Grand Council's Military Affairs Committee stated only that 'discrepancies in personnel records during active conflict are not uncommon' and declined to address the specific cases I named. But the non-commissioned officers I've cultivated as sources over nine years are talking to each other now, and what they're saying matches what I've suspected since those prisoners in Ironkeep began reciting the Grand Army oath in unison: whatever the Shadow Legion has become, it is wearing our faces.

Surgeon Captain Greymark asked me, before I left her tent, whether I thought the public deserved to know. I told her what I tell every source who asks that question: the public deserves honest witnesses, especially when the truth is something no one wants to hear. She nodded once, then returned to her work. There are more dead coming in tonight. Some of them, I suspect, we will have already buried once before.
