Sealed Vanthorn Case Files Reveal Third Witness Was City Watch Informant for Twelve Years

Dead clerk's personal archive contained documentation showing systematic Watch intelligence sharing with crime syndicate targets.

NX
By Nyx Shadowmere Crime & Justice Reporter
24 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The court clerk found dead in the sealed archive room last week was not merely a records custodian. Henric Ashwell maintained a parallel archive in a rented storage unit in the Veil District—one containing twelve years of documentation proving he served as an intelligence conduit between City Watch investigators and the very criminal organizations those investigators were supposedly pursuing. I have reviewed forty-seven of those documents. They fundamentally alter our understanding of the Vanthorn smuggling case and the systematic witness deaths that have followed it.

Ashwell's private records include signed receipts for payments totaling 4,200 crowns over the past three years, each bearing a symbol my sources within the criminal courts have confirmed as an Obsidian Ring authentication mark. More damning still: the archive contains copies of sealed testimony schedules, witness protection assignments, and internal Watch investigation timelines—documents that would have allowed anyone reading them to know precisely where protected witnesses would be and when they would be most vulnerable. The three witnesses who vanished before their scheduled testimony in the Vanthorn case? Their safe house assignments appear in Ashwell's records, dated six days before they disappeared.

Magistrate Thornwood, who has been managing the increasingly deadly Vanthorn proceedings, confirmed to me that she was unaware of Ashwell's dual role. 'I trusted the integrity of my court's administrative staff,' she said in a brief statement provided through her clerk. 'That trust was apparently misplaced, and I am now reviewing every case Henric Ashwell touched during his tenure.' That review will encompass hundreds of criminal proceedings spanning more than a decade—cases where defendants may have had advance knowledge of prosecution strategies, witness identities, and evidence inventories.

The discovery raises urgent questions about how Ashwell operated undetected for so long within a system designed to protect sensitive information. My sources indicate that Watch Internal Affairs—the same unit disbanded three days ago after its lead investigator was found with an Obsidian Ring contract—had received anonymous complaints about courthouse security breaches as early as Year 843 AP. Those complaints were filed and never investigated. The officer assigned to review them appears on the recently discovered ledger of Watch personnel receiving Obsidian Ring payments.

I have spent six years building the map of corruption that connects the City Watch, the court system, and the criminal networks they were meant to oppose. Ashwell's archive does not complete that map, but it illuminates connections I had only suspected. The Vanthorn case was never simply about smuggling—it was a thread that, when pulled, threatened to unravel protections that powerful people have spent years constructing. The witnesses are dead or dying because they could name those people. Ashwell is dead because his archive could prove the names were accurate. Someone is cleaning house with methodical efficiency, and the bodies are accumulating faster than justice can count them.

Investigator Rael Mourne, my former Watch partner and the only serving officer I trust completely, has agreed to review Ashwell's documents under conditions I will not disclose. The storage unit has been secured. The contents have been duplicated. Whatever happens next, the evidence will survive—even if the people who gathered it do not. This is the twenty-third death connected to the Vanthorn proceedings. I do not expect it will be the last.
