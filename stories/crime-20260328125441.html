Founding Hero of Seldîré Accused of Forbidden Necromancy, Vanishes After Tower Confrontation

Vindil, one of the legendary seven who slayed the Thornscale Dragon and united the Elvish Kingdom, threw himself from his tower rather than face arrest. His body has not been recovered.

NX
By Nyx Shadowmere Crime & Justice Reporter
28 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

THORNHAVEN BORDER REGION — One of the seven legendary founders of the Elvish Kingdom of Seldîré is dead or missing after a confrontation with Rangers revealed he had been practicing necromancy, the forbidden art of manipulating the dead, in the isolated tower where he had lived in near-total seclusion for centuries. Vindil, whose name appears on the founding charter alongside six companions who together slayed the Thornscale Dragon and united the scattered elvish villages into a single prosperous kingdom, threw himself from the tower's highest window rather than submit to arrest. Despite extensive searches by Ranger units and tracking mages, his body has not been recovered.

The investigation began after Elbreth, a young apprentice Vindil had accepted barely a year ago—his first student in living memory—reported concerns about her master's practices to the Head Minister of Seldîré. According to court documents filed in the elvish capital, Elbreth observed rituals and materials in Vindil's private chambers that she recognized from forbidden texts. One of the surviving founders personally led an elite Ranger team to investigate, and what they discovered in that tower confirmed the apprentice's account. The specific nature of Vindil's necromantic work remains classified by order of the remaining founders, though sources within the Ranger corps describe the evidence as 'extensive and undeniable.'

The confrontation itself was brief but violent. Witnesses report that Vindil unleashed defensive spells against the Rangers before retreating to the tower's uppermost level. When the founder who led the investigation attempted to negotiate his surrender, Vindil reportedly spoke words that no one present has been willing to repeat, then stepped backward through the window. The fall should have been fatal. The absence of a body has prompted speculation ranging from teleportation magic to darker theories that his necromantic studies may have provided some means of survival. Elbreth was unharmed during the incident, and no Rangers suffered serious injuries despite the magical assault.

The revelation has sent shockwaves through both Seldîré and the broader diplomatic community that maintains relations with the elvish kingdom. 'He was a founder,' said Merchant Liaison Aldric Thornwood, who has negotiated trade agreements with Seldîré for fifteen years. 'When I was a boy, my grandmother told me stories of the Seven who faced down a dragon to save villages that couldn't save themselves. To learn that one of them turned to death magic—it makes you wonder what you can trust.' In Thornhaven, where elvish and human cultures have mixed since the Treaty of Year 801 AP, residents gathered in the Halfwood market square to discuss the news with a mixture of disbelief and grief.

The six surviving founders have issued a joint statement acknowledging Vindil's betrayal while requesting privacy to mourn their former companion. 'We fought beside him. We trusted him with our lives and with the future of our people,' the statement read in part. 'That trust was violated, and we must now reckon with the fact that even those we love most deeply can lose their way.' Officials have announced that murals and monuments depicting the Seven across Seldîré will be 'adjusted to reflect current realities,' though the precise nature of those adjustments has not been specified. Some citizens have called for Vindil's image to be removed entirely; others argue that erasing history serves no one.

What remains unclear is how long Vindil practiced forbidden magic in his tower, and whether his famous reclusiveness—he had withdrawn almost entirely from public life since the kingdom's founding, rarely appearing even for ceremonies honoring the Seven—was a symptom of corruption already taking root, or simply the personality that eventually made corruption possible. The remaining founders have not commented on when they last spoke with him, or whether they noticed any warning signs. For now, the Ranger corps continues its search for a body that may never be found, while a kingdom built on the heroism of seven founders learns to tell its story with only six.
