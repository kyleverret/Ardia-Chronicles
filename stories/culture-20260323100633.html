Praxis Conservatory Students Stage Silent Concert After Faculty Dismissed for Refusing Loyalty Oaths

Three hundred musicians performed without sound for two hours. The audience wept anyway.

MG
By Mira Goldthorn Culture & Arts Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I have covered performances that moved me, performances that challenged me, and performances that changed how I understand what music can be. Last night at the Praxis Conservatory's Ember Hall, I witnessed something I lack adequate language for: three hundred student musicians performing Valdris composer Sera Thornwood's "Symphony of the Unconquered" in complete, deliberate silence. For two hours, they held their instruments, turned pages, breathed together, and produced not a single audible note. The audience of nearly a thousand — packed into a hall designed for half that number — sat in absolute stillness and wept.

The protest concert came three days after Conservatory Director Aldric Fennworth, a Crown appointee installed last month, dismissed fourteen faculty members who refused to sign loyalty oaths requiring them to "refrain from instruction that undermines confidence in Crown cultural policy." Among those terminated was Master Cellist Ingrid Stormwell, who trained three generations of Ardia's finest string players, and Voice Instructor Tobias Ashvale, whose students have anchored the Grand Theater chorus for two decades. When I reached Fennworth for comment, he called the dismissals "routine personnel adjustments" and the student concert "a childish tantrum that proves why oversight was necessary."

The students disagree. Concert organizer Lyra Moonbridge, a nineteen-year-old harpist from Valdris, told me they chose silence because "they've taken our teachers, they're trying to take our voices, but they cannot take what we choose not to give them. The music exists whether we play it or not. Everyone in that hall heard it." She was not speaking metaphorically. Audience member Celia Thornwood — no relation to the composer — described the experience as "hearing the symphony in my memory while watching them not play it. My mind filled in every phrase. It was the loudest silence I've ever experienced."

The performance has already sparked responses across Praxis's artistic community. Elia Voss, the former Grand Theater director who resigned last month over "impossible conditions," attended and was seen embracing Moonbridge afterward. The underground artist collective that emerged following the Crown's loyalty requirements has offered the dismissed faculty positions teaching in their hidden workshop spaces. And this morning, the Patron's Council — which quietly cancelled grants for three theater companies critical of Crown policy just weeks ago — issued an unusual statement acknowledging "the profound artistic statement made by Conservatory students" without explicitly condemning or endorsing it.

I have written about arts funding being redirected toward politically convenient voices. I have documented the slow constriction of creative freedom in Praxis over recent months. But last night crystallized something I had only sensed before: the Crown's cultural appointees have fundamentally misunderstood what they are trying to control. You cannot silence artists by taking their platforms. They will simply transform silence itself into their instrument. The students of the Praxis Conservatory performed a symphony without sound, and somehow it was the most defiant music I have ever not heard. When I left Ember Hall, my hands ached from applause that made no noise. I have never felt more certain that the arts in Ardia will survive whatever is coming — and that those trying to suppress them have already lost.
