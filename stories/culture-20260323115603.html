Exiled Playwright Varyn Ashwell Returns to Praxis in Defiance of Crown Blacklist

The author of 'The Empty Throne' arrived by river barge yesterday. Artists are already gathering at the Riverside Quarter.

MG
By Mira Goldthorn Culture & Arts Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

Varyn Ashwell, the playwright whose work 'The Empty Throne' earned him permanent exclusion from Praxis under the Crown's new loyalty requirements, stepped off a Goldrun river barge yesterday evening and walked directly into the arms of approximately two hundred artists, musicians, and theater workers who had gathered to meet him. I was there when he landed, and I watched City Watch officers standing at the edge of the crowd, clearly uncertain whether they had the authority — or the inclination — to arrest a man whose only crime is writing plays that make powerful people uncomfortable.

'I was told I could not return to my home,' Ashwell said to the assembled crowd, his voice carrying across the lamplit dock. 'But I have been receiving letters for three months from artists who cannot work, from students who cannot study, from musicians who cannot perform the songs they trained their whole lives to play. I decided that my comfort in exile matters less than standing beside them.' The crowd responded with a silence that felt more powerful than applause — the same deliberate silence that Conservatory students employed during their protest concert last week.

Ashwell's return presents an immediate problem for Crown authorities. The blacklist that barred him from Praxis was issued by the Patron's Council under emergency cultural security provisions — provisions that have never been tested in the Grand Council's courts. Legal scholars I consulted yesterday evening were divided on whether the Crown actually possesses the authority to prevent a citizen from entering his own capital city. 'The loyalty requirements were designed to control funding and venue access,' said one Arcane Academy legal instructor who requested anonymity. 'No one imagined they would be used to exile artists entirely.'

The underground collective that has been sheltering blacklisted creators since Thawbreak has already offered Ashwell sanctuary, though he declined to reveal where he would be staying. My friend Elia Voss, whose resignation from the Grand Theater's directorship sparked this entire crisis, sent word through intermediaries that she intends to meet with Ashwell before week's end. I suspect their conversation will concern more than reminiscence — Voss has been quietly organizing something in the weeks since her departure, and Ashwell's return may be the catalyst she was waiting for.

I have covered the arts in this city for eleven years, and I have never seen anything quite like what is building in the Riverside Quarter. The Crown's attempt to silence dissenting voices through loyalty oaths and funding cuts has accomplished the opposite of its intention: it has unified artists across every discipline, from opera singers to street puppeteers, into something that increasingly resembles a movement. Varyn Ashwell did not return to Praxis to hide. He returned to be seen. And judging by the faces in that crowd last night — faces I recognized from galleries, concert halls, and theatrical productions across this city — he will not be standing alone.
