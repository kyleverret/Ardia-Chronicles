Underground Artist Collective Reveals Crown Loyalty Officers Planted False Evidence Against Blacklisted Creators

Seized documents show systematic fabrication of 'seditious content' used to justify seventeen artist arrests since Frosthold.

MG
By Mira Goldthorn Culture & Arts Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The Lamplighters' Circle—the underground collective that has sheltered blacklisted artists since the Crown's loyalty requirements took effect—has released a cache of documents that appear to show Crown Loyalty Officers systematically planting fabricated evidence in the workshops and homes of artists targeted for arrest. The documents, which I have spent the past three days verifying through multiple independent sources, include handwritten instructions for creating false 'seditious manuscripts' in specific artists' handwriting styles, along with receipts for materials purchased through a Crown administrative account.

Among those whose arrests now appear to have been manufactured: Celeste Ironwood, the ceramicist whose studio was raided in early Frosthold after Watch officers claimed to find anti-Council pamphlets hidden in unfired clay vessels. Ironwood, who is seventy-three years old and has never expressed political opinions in any public forum, told me from her current hiding place that the pamphlets were written in a dialect she does not speak. 'I make teacups,' she said, her hands still bearing the calluses of forty years at the wheel. 'I have made teacups my entire life. The idea that I was printing revolutionary materials is absurd to anyone who has ever met me.'

The documents trace back to an office within the Royal Court's cultural appointments division—the same office where my source first alerted me to the quiet redirection of arts funding eighteen months ago. What began as patronage politics has apparently evolved into something far more sinister: a coordinated effort to criminalize artists whose only offense was failing to sign loyalty oaths they found unconscionable. The Praxis Conservatory faculty members dismissed last week, the theater companies whose grants were cancelled, the Opera House performers now in hiding—all of them appear in planning documents dated months before any public loyalty requirements were announced.

I reached out to the Royal Court's cultural appointments office for comment. A junior secretary informed me that all inquiries regarding 'cultural security matters' must be submitted in writing and would receive response 'within the appropriate timeframe.' When I asked what that timeframe might be, she ended the communication. The Patron's Council, with whom I have maintained a complicated but functional relationship for a decade, has not responded to three separate message attempts since yesterday morning—a silence that speaks volumes from an organization that has never before declined to defend itself.

Elia Voss, the Grand Theater director whose resignation over 'impossible conditions' helped expose the scope of Crown interference in the arts, told me via secure messenger that the documents confirm what many suspected but could not prove. 'They did not want loyal artists,' she wrote. 'They wanted silent ones. And when silence was not enough, they wanted prisoners.' The Lamplighters' Circle has announced it will continue releasing documents daily until the full scope of the operation is public. I will be here to verify each one. The artists of Praxis have been accused of sedition for the crime of making art without permission. It is time we learned exactly who manufactured those accusations, and why.
