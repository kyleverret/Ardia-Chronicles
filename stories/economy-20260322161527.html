Merchants' Guild Ledgers Reveal Secret Payments to Crown Officials During Crystal Stockpiling Period

Leaked documents show fourteen Council members received monthly stipends from shell company linked to frozen magitech firms.

ES
By Elara Sunforge Business & Economy Editor
22 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I have spent fifteen years reading financial documents, and I have never seen corruption laid out so plainly as in the forty-seven pages that arrived at my desk yesterday morning. The package bore no return address, only a wax seal I recognized from the Merchants' Guild's internal audit division — a seal that should never appear on documents leaving guild headquarters. Inside were monthly payment records spanning eighteen months, detailing regular transfers from an entity called Ardian Futures Holdings to fourteen sitting members of the Grand Council, including three who serve on the treasury sub-committee that the vanished Councilor Maren Ashworth once chaired.

Ardian Futures Holdings does not exist in any public registry. However, cross-referencing account numbers with records I obtained during last week's banking freeze investigation reveals direct financial connections to four of the six magitech firms whose assets were frozen for suspicious crystal stockpiling. The payments began precisely two months before the crystal market volatility I first reported in January and continued until three weeks ago — stopping abruptly the day after my story on the Merchants' Guild emergency session ran on our front page. Someone knew scrutiny was coming and closed the spigot.

The amounts are not trivial. Councilor Aldric Pemberton — the same official who refused to provide the third signature on Ashworth's audit request — received 847 gold sovereigns monthly, more than triple his official Council salary. Treasury liaison Vera Castellan received 612 sovereigns. The remaining twelve recipients hold positions on committees overseeing trade regulation, mining rights, and arcane commerce. "These are not gifts," said Guildmaster Theron Ashford of the Banking Guild's compliance division, who reviewed the documents at my request. "These are employment payments. Someone was buying policy outcomes."

I contacted all fourteen named officials before publication. Eleven declined to respond. Two claimed the documents were forgeries, though neither would submit to a verification interview. Councilor Pemberton's office issued a three-sentence statement calling my inquiry "economically illiterate speculation" — words I find professionally irritating from a man whose signature is clearly visible on seventeen separate payment acknowledgments.

What troubles me most is the timing. These payments coincide precisely with Council votes that relaxed oversight of magitech investment, expanded crystal export permits, and — most significantly — delayed mandatory audits of the Crown Reserve Vaults that Councilor Ashworth was investigating when she disappeared. I cannot yet prove that her vanishing is connected to these ledgers, but I can state with certainty that someone spent a great deal of money ensuring the treasury remained unexamined. The question I intend to answer next is simple: whose money was it, and what were they hiding in those vaults?
