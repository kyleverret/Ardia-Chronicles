Banking Guild Compliance Chief Resigns After Discovering His Own Signature on Forged Crown Reserve Withdrawals

Theron Ashford says documents bearing his name authorized transfers he never approved. Guild launches internal investigation.

ES
By Elara Sunforge Business & Economy Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

Theron Ashford, the Banking Guild's senior compliance officer and one of the most respected financial regulators in Ardia, submitted his resignation yesterday after temple auditors showed him withdrawal authorizations from the Crown Reserve Vaults bearing his signature — documents he swears he never signed. The discovery throws the ongoing treasury investigation into chaos and raises alarming questions about how deeply the forgery operation has penetrated the realm's financial institutions.

"I have spent nineteen years building a reputation for integrity," Ashford told me in an interview at his Praxis residence, where he has remained since leaving Guild headquarters. "Someone used that reputation as a weapon. Every signature matches my hand exactly — the pressure points, the slight upward stroke on the final letter. But I have never authorized a single one of these withdrawals." The documents in question cover transfers totaling approximately 127,000 gold sovereigns over a three-year period, all routed to accounts that temple auditors have now linked to Ardian Futures Holdings — the same shell company implicated in payments to fourteen Council members.

Guild Master Ressa Thornwood announced an immediate internal investigation but stopped short of accepting Ashford's resignation. "Compliance Officer Ashford reported this discrepancy himself the moment he became aware of it," she said in a statement. "That is not the behavior of someone complicit in fraud." My sources within the Banking Guild suggest the forged signatures may have been created using a specialized form of script-mimicry enchantment — illegal under the Arcane Practice Act but available through certain unlicensed practitioners in the Thornhaven black markets.

The implications extend far beyond Ashford's personal reputation. If someone possessed the capability to forge signatures convincing enough to pass Banking Guild verification, the entire authorization framework governing Crown accounts must be considered compromised. I have confirmed that Magistrate Thornwood has requested an emergency review of every withdrawal document from the Reserve Vaults dating back to Year 840 AP — approximately two thousand individual transactions that must now be re-verified through temple truth-reading protocols.

Ashford's name appeared in my reporting last week as a source raising concerns about financial connections between frozen magitech firms and active mining operations. I asked him whether he believes his cooperation with the Chronicle made him a target. He was quiet for a long moment before answering. "Someone needed a credible signature on fraudulent documents," he said. "I have been publicly involved in compliance oversight for nearly two decades. Perhaps the real question is how many other respected names have been borrowed without their owners' knowledge — and what else those names have been made to approve."
