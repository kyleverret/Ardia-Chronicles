Crystal Exchange Halts All Trading After Discovery of Counterfeit Power Cells in Certified Vault Storage

Exchange officials estimate up to 40,000 cells may be affected. Merchants' Guild demands immediate investigation into certification fraud.

ES
By Elara Sunforge Business & Economy Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The Crystal Exchange suspended all trading at midday yesterday after vault inspectors discovered that approximately 40,000 certified power cells in bonded storage may be sophisticated counterfeits — forgeries good enough to pass standard guild verification but containing unstable resonance cores that could fail catastrophically under normal use. The suspension marks the first complete halt of crystal trading since the Shadow Legion crisis of Year 844 AP, and it has sent shockwaves through every industry that depends on reliable power cell supply.

Exchange Director Aldric Stonehammer announced the suspension from the trading floor steps, his voice steady but his hands visibly shaking as he addressed the crowd of merchants and traders. "We discovered the irregularity during a routine storage rotation this morning," he said. "The affected cells bear authentic-appearing certification stamps from the Artificers' Guild and passed our standard resonance screening. However, deep-spectrum analysis reveals crystalline structures that do not match any licensed Greyward extraction." When pressed on how counterfeit cells entered certified storage, Stonehammer declined to speculate, saying only that the Banking Guild and City Watch have been notified.

The implications extend far beyond the trading floor. Crystal power cells fuel everything from ArcLight illumination networks to runescript communication systems to the enchanted transport carriages that connect Praxis to Valdris. If counterfeit cells have entered general circulation — and several merchants I spoke with yesterday believe they have — the realm faces not merely a financial crisis but a potential infrastructure emergency. My sources within CrystalCore Industries confirmed that the company has begun emergency testing of its own inventory, though a spokesperson declined to comment on preliminary findings.

The timing is deeply troubling. With legitimate Greyward production already disrupted by ley-line instabilities, crystal prices had risen 28 percent since Frosthold. That price spike created precisely the conditions in which counterfeit goods thrive — high demand, limited supply, and desperate buyers willing to overlook irregularities. I have traced the vault storage records for the suspect cells back through three separate trading houses, all of which received investment within the past eighteen months from Ardian Futures Holdings, the shell company now at the center of the Crown Reserve investigation. When I presented this connection to Director Stonehammer, he said only, "The pattern you're describing would suggest coordination beyond anything we've seen in my thirty years on this Exchange."

Merchants' Guild President Aldra Copperhand has called for an emergency session tomorrow to demand full disclosure of every power cell that passed through bonded storage in the past two years — a logistical undertaking that could take months and cost thousands of crowns. Meanwhile, the small traders and shop owners who bought cells in good faith now face an impossible choice: use inventory that might be dangerous, or absorb losses they cannot afford. "I have twelve cells I purchased last month for my bakery's heating array," said Millicent Ashford, whose shop on Silver Street has used crystal-powered ovens for a decade. "Are they going to explode? Are they worthless? No one can tell me." The Exchange has provided no timeline for resuming trading, and until the scope of the counterfeiting operation is known, the crystal market — and every business that depends on it — remains frozen in uncertainty.
