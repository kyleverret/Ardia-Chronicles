Merchants' Guild Emergency Audit Reveals 340,000 Crowns in Payments to Shell Companies Now Linked to Counterfeit Cell Network

Guild President Aldra Copperhand suspends twelve senior members pending investigation. Treasury officials demand access to private ledgers.

ES
By Elara Sunforge Business & Economy Editor
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The Merchants' Guild's internal emergency audit, initiated following last week's Crystal Exchange trading halt, has uncovered approximately 340,000 crowns in payments routed to shell companies now confirmed as distribution points for the counterfeit power cells that paralyzed crystal markets across Ardia. Guild President Aldra Copperhand announced the suspension of twelve senior guild members yesterday evening, her voice carrying clearly through the packed assembly hall as merchants and traders pressed against the gallery rails to hear her address. "These payments were disguised as legitimate certification fees and storage contracts," Copperhand said. "They were not. They were facilitation payments for what appears to be the largest counterfeiting operation in guild history."

My examination of the audit documents—copies of which were provided by a source within the guild's compliance office—reveals a pattern that extends the Ardian Futures Holdings scandal directly into the realm's primary commercial institution. Eight of the twelve suspended members received investment funding from intermediary accounts that my Banking Guild contacts have traced through the same channels used to establish the shell companies at the center of the Crown Reserve investigation. The payments began eighteen months ago, approximately three months before the first shipments of suspect power cells appeared in bonded Exchange storage. When I presented this timeline to former Banking Guild Compliance Chief Aldric Fenn—who resigned last week after discovering his forged signature on Crown Reserve withdrawal documents—he said only, "Someone built this very carefully, over a very long time. The question is whether they built it for profit or for something worse."

The Treasury Sub-Committee's acting chair, Councillor Maren Ashby of Valdris, has formally demanded access to the guild's private ledgers under the emergency provisions of the Grand Council's Commercial Oversight Act. Guild President Copperhand has thus far resisted, arguing that the demand exceeds the Sub-Committee's authority and sets a dangerous precedent for government intrusion into private commercial associations. "The guild is conducting its own investigation," she told me via runescript last night. "We do not require the Council's assistance, nor do we intend to submit our members' private business arrangements to political scrutiny during an election cycle." Councillor Ashby's response was characteristically direct: "When guild members are funneling crowns to the same shell companies that emptied the Crown Reserve, political scrutiny is precisely what the citizens of Ardia deserve."

The twelve suspended members include three who hold seats on the Crystal Exchange's certification board—the body responsible for verifying the authenticity of every power cell that enters bonded storage. Exchange Director Aldric Stonehammer declined to comment on whether this connection explains how 40,000 counterfeit cells passed standard guild verification, but a source within his office confirmed that the Exchange is now reviewing every certification decision made in the past two years. "We trusted our own board," the source said. "That may have been the most expensive mistake the Exchange has ever made."

Meanwhile, the economic consequences continue to cascade. With crystal trading still suspended and legitimate Greyward production disrupted by ongoing ley-line instabilities, businesses across Praxis are rationing power cell usage. ArcLight Illumination has reduced street lamp operation in three districts to preserve reserves, and Runescript Networks has warned customers of potential service delays if the Exchange does not resume operations within the week. Small merchants who purchased what they believed were certified cells now face an impossible calculus: absorb losses they cannot afford, risk using inventory that might fail catastrophically, or close their doors entirely until the market stabilizes. I spoke with seventeen shop owners on Silver Street yesterday. Eleven are considering closure. "I built this bakery with my grandmother's copper bracelet as collateral," Millicent Ashford told me, her crystal-powered ovens cold for the third consecutive day. "Now I'm supposed to trust that the same guild that certified these counterfeits will somehow make things right?" She shook her head. "The guild looks after its own. The rest of us look after our empty shelves."

The connection between counterfeit power cells, shell company payments, and the broader Crown Reserve scandal grows clearer with each passing day—and with each revelation, the question of who orchestrated this systematic looting of Ardia's financial infrastructure becomes more urgent. I have spent fifteen years building sources within the Merchants' Guild, and I have never seen senior members this frightened. Whatever they know about where those 340,000 crowns ultimately went, they are not yet willing to say it publicly. But the ledgers do not lie, and the trail they reveal leads somewhere. My investigation continues.
