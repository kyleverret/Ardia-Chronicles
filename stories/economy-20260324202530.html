Crystal Exchange Forensic Audit Reveals 23 Certification Board Decisions Were Made After Board Members Had Already Died

Impossible timestamps on power cell approvals suggest systematic forgery predates the counterfeit scandal by at least eighteen months.

ES
By Elara Sunforge Business & Economy Editor
24 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I have spent fifteen years reading financial documents, and I have learned that ledgers do not lie—but the people who write them certainly do. Yesterday's forensic audit of the Crystal Exchange's certification records revealed something that transforms our understanding of the counterfeit power cell scandal: twenty-three official certification approvals bear the signatures of board members who were, according to Guild death registries and verified burial records, already deceased at the time of signing. The earliest impossible signature dates to Harvestmoon of Year 845 AP—eighteen months before the first counterfeit cells entered bonded storage.

Exchange Director Aldric Stonehammer confirmed the findings in a tersely worded statement released this morning, calling the discovery 'a fundamental breach of institutional integrity that predates my tenure.' The deceased signatories include former Certification Board Chair Matthias Ironwright, whose distinctive seal appears on fourteen separate approvals issued between four and eleven months after his death from crystal lung in Brightmantle 845 AP. His widow, Elspeth Ironwright, told me by runescript that she surrendered his official seal to the Exchange the day after his funeral. 'Someone in that building took my husband's name and used it to approve things he never saw,' she said. 'I want to know who, and I want them to answer for it.'

The implications extend far beyond institutional embarrassment. Certification board approvals are the foundation of trust in the crystal market—every power cell that enters bonded storage must bear a board member's seal confirming it meets purity and safety standards. If those seals were being forged eighteen months ago, the contamination of Ardia's power cell supply may be far more extensive than the 40,000 counterfeit units already identified. My sources within the Merchants' Guild estimate that between 200,000 and 400,000 additional cells certified during the window of impossible signatures remain in circulation, their authenticity now fundamentally uncertain.

I have obtained records showing that the forged certifications cluster around shipments from three Greyward mining operations—all of which, I can now confirm, received investment capital from shell companies linked to Ardian Futures Holdings, the same entity implicated in the Crown Reserve vault discrepancies. The pattern suggests the certification forgery was not opportunistic fraud but infrastructure: someone needed a reliable method to move uncertified crystal into bonded storage before they began producing counterfeits at scale. The dead board members' seals provided that method.

Magistrate Thornwood has expanded her investigation to include the Exchange's records archive, where the original certification documents are stored. A source within her office confirmed that preliminary examination suggests the forged seals were applied using genuine impression stamps—meaning either the Exchange failed to secure deceased members' equipment, or someone with archive access retrieved the stamps after surrender. Neither explanation reflects well on an institution that has spent a century asking Ardia to trust its word. The crystal market remains suspended. Legitimate merchants continue to suffer. And somewhere in this city, the architects of what may be the most elaborate financial fraud in Ardian history are watching us piece together what they built, one impossible signature at a time.
