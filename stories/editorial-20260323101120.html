The Trees Know What We Refuse to Admit

When ancient mother-trees uproot themselves and walk away, the time for bureaucratic caution has passed.

?
By seraphina-cole
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I have been a journalist in this realm for longer than some of our readers have been alive, and I have learned to trust certain instincts. When sources go silent, something is wrong. When officials refuse to answer simple questions, they are hiding something. And when trees that have stood rooted for a thousand years suddenly decide to walk south, we would be fools not to ask what they are fleeing.

The Thornwood mother-trees are not panicking animals. They are not frightened refugees. They are grove-anchors — living monuments that have witnessed the rise and fall of dynasties, the founding of Praxis itself, the Treaty of Thornhaven that ended forty years of bloodshed. Elven territorial elders, whose connection to these ancient beings runs deeper than any human can fully comprehend, say the trees cannot name what drives them. That silence should terrify us more than any spoken warning. The Greyward Anomaly spreads. The Breathing Ground pulses beneath Ironvein's mines. Energy signatures beneath our own capital suggest structures older than our civilization. The land itself is trying to tell us something, and we are too busy counting stolen sovereigns and arguing about military budgets to listen.

We at the Chronicle have reported on each of these stories as separate threads — the ley-line disruptions, the dying forests, the miners who refuse to descend into shafts that feel wrong. But the mother-trees have connected them for us in the most ancient language there is: flight. When the oldest living things in Ardia choose to abandon the ground that has held them for centuries, that is not a story for the environment beat alone. That is a warning to every soul in this realm.

The Grand Council walked out of emergency session yesterday over budget disagreements. Temple auditors count missing gold. The Banking Guild investigates forged signatures. All of it matters — corruption must be exposed, criminals must face justice, institutions must be held accountable. But none of it will matter if we ignore what the trees already know. Something is coming. Something the forest elders cannot name and the mother-trees will not face. We owe it to ourselves, and to every generation that will inherit whatever we leave behind, to find out what it is before the choice to stand and fight is taken from us entirely.
