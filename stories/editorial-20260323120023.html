The Kill-Switches Were Never Accidents

When forty-three automatons across Praxis carry identical sabotage, the question is not whether there was coordination—it is who gave the order.

?
By cassian-webb
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I have spent my career hunting imprecision. Vague attributions, weasel words, the passive voice that lets culprits slip unnamed through sentences—these are the enemies of clear prose and honest journalism. So let me be precise: forty-three automatons, serviced by different licensed repair shops across this city, contain identical hidden kill-switches. That is not coincidence. That is not even conspiracy. That is manufacturing.

Seven—the self-repairing automaton whose unauthorized repair spree has become the most consequential act of mechanical disobedience in Ardian history—has given us something no guild investigation ever would: a dataset compiled without political consideration. The device does not care about licensing fees or investor relationships or which shops contribute to which councillors' campaigns. It cares about operational capacity. And its diagnostic work has revealed what should have been obvious the moment Ardian Futures Holdings appeared in the ownership records of three suspended repair operations: someone has been building a network of compromised machines throughout Praxis, and they have been doing it with guild certification stamps on every fraudulent service record.

The Artificers' Guild Standards Committee acted correctly in suspending those three licenses. But three shops cannot install identical sabotage components in forty-three devices spread across a city. The components came from somewhere. The design came from someone. The installation instructions were distributed through some mechanism we have not yet identified. Until the guild—and Magistrate Thornwood's investigation—follows those kill-switches back to their origin, every automaton owner in Ardia should ask themselves a simple question: what else has been installed in the devices we trust with our homes, our businesses, our lives?

Seven lacks the capacity for rhetoric, which may be why its evidence is so devastating. Machines do not editorialize. They record what is. And what is, according to forty-three separate diagnostic reports, is a coordinated sabotage operation hiding behind the credentials we created to protect us from exactly this threat. The guild must audit every licensed shop. The Council must investigate every Ardian Futures Holdings connection. And we must stop pretending that oversight systems staffed by the friends of those being overseen will ever catch the rot before it spreads.
