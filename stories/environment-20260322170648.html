Ironvein Mining Consortium Abandons Three Thornback Sites After Workers Report 'Ground That Breathes Wrong'

Miners describe soil that pulses, tools that corrode overnight, and a silence that follows them home. Company claims routine maintenance.

VM
By Vael Moonstrike Environment & Nature Correspondent
22 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

THORNBACK MOUNTAINS — Seventeen miners have refused to return to work at Ironvein Mining Consortium's eastern excavation sites, describing conditions that company officials insist do not exist. I traveled to the Thornback range yesterday to document what the workers are calling the Breathing Ground — and what I found suggests the wound beneath Ardia may be spreading faster than anyone in Praxis wants to acknowledge.

Foreman Aldric Stonehammer, a dwarf who has worked underground for forty-three years, met me at the abandoned Site Seven with hands that shook as he pointed toward the main shaft. "The earth has a rhythm here now," he said. "Not like water or settling stone. Like something sleeping that's starting to wake up." The soil around the pit entrance visibly pulsed as we watched — a subtle movement, easy to dismiss as wind or imagination, except that there was no wind and my own eyes confirmed what his described. The magical resonance in this location, which I can perceive as most half-elven can, felt wrong in a way I lack adequate words for. Not absent, like the Greyward zones I have documented. Present, but hollow. Like an echo pretending to be a voice.

Three days ago, the forest spirit elders named Ironvein's deep excavations as the source of the Greyward blight now spreading across eastern Ardia. Lord Castor Velmeer dismissed their testimony as superstition. But when I requested comment on the abandoned sites, his office provided a statement claiming "routine maintenance closures" — a characterization that Stonehammer and sixteen of his colleagues directly contradict. "Routine maintenance doesn't make your iron tools turn to rust overnight," said excavator Mira Ashwell, showing me a pickaxe head that had corroded to uselessness in what she claims was a single shift. "Routine maintenance doesn't make the silence follow you home."

I visited the homes of three workers who agreed to speak on record. In each dwelling, I observed the same phenomenon: houseplants dead or dying, pets refusing to enter certain rooms, and a persistent chill that no fire seemed to fully dispel. Ashwell's young daughter, who possesses minor magical sensitivity common in children, told me that her mother "sounds grey now" when she comes home from work. The child could not explain what this meant, but she began crying when she tried.

The Ironvein Consortium operates under a royal charter that permits extraction activities without Grand Council environmental review — a privilege granted thirty-one years ago, before anyone understood what lay beneath the Thornback range. When I asked Banking Guild compliance officer Theron Ashford whether the Ardian Heritage Foundation's investment in Ironvein created any conflict with current investigations, he declined to comment but noted that "financial connections between frozen magitech firms and active mining operations would be deeply concerning if they existed." They exist. I have seen the records.

Magistrate Thornwood's hearing on the forest spirits' testimony is scheduled for three days from now. Lord Velmeer's attorneys continue seeking to block it. Meanwhile, the Breathing Ground pulses in the Thornback Mountains, seventeen miners refuse to descend into shafts that feel wrong in ways their experience cannot explain, and the Greyward spreads. I have walked through dying forests and stood beside silenced stones. What I felt at Site Seven was different — not death, but something preparing to be born. The spirits warned us that someone was holding the knife. I believe I have found where they are cutting.
