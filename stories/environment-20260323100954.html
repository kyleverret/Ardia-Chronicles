Ancient Thornwood Mother-Trees Begin Walking South for First Time in Recorded History

Forest wardens report three grove-anchors have uprooted themselves. Elven territorial elders say the trees are fleeing something they cannot name.

VM
By Vael Moonstrike Environment & Nature Correspondent
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

THORNWOOD BORDER REGION — I arrived at the Thornwood's northern edge yesterday morning expecting to document the spread of the Greyward blight into another ancient forest. What I found instead has no precedent in the four thousand years of recorded Ardian natural history: three Mother-Trees, the grove-anchors that have stood rooted in the same soil since before humans built their first settlements here, are walking.

The Mother-Trees are not ordinary growth. They are the oldest living beings in Ardia — massive oaks and silverwoods whose root systems extend for miles beneath the forest floor, connecting and sustaining entire ecosystems. They do not move. They have never moved. The Treaty of Thornhaven, signed forty-six years ago to end the border wars, was negotiated beneath a Mother-Tree specifically because both human and elven diplomats understood that nothing could be more permanent. That tree, called Grandmother Ashwind by the elven communities, lifted her roots from the earth three days ago and began traveling south at approximately one hundred yards per day.

"She spoke to me before she moved," said Warden-Elder Caelindra of the Thornwood Grove Council, the first elven territorial official to agree to speak with human press about the phenomenon. "Not in words. In feeling. The closest translation I can offer is: 'The ground remembers wrongness now. I will not stay to be remembered with it.'" Caelindra, who has served as grove warden for over two centuries, wept as she described watching Grandmother Ashwind's departure. The forest spirits who normally communicate through the Mother-Trees have gone silent since the walking began.

I followed Grandmother Ashwind's trail for nearly a mile yesterday afternoon, accompanied by three members of the Forest Warden's Guild. The path she left behind is not destruction — smaller trees and undergrowth appear unharmed — but the soil itself has changed. My warden's cord, which normally pulses faintly with natural resonance when I enter old-growth territory, went cold against my wrist. The magical signature of the land she walked across has been altered in ways my training cannot fully identify. It felt, I must report, like the Breathing Ground that Ironvein miners described at their abandoned Thornback sites. Not dead. Not alive. Waiting.

The Grand Council's Land and Resources Committee has issued no statement on the walking Mother-Trees. Lord Castor Velmeer, whose Ironvein Mining Consortium the forest spirit elders blamed for the Greyward blight, has reportedly requested an emergency session to discuss "irrational panic over natural seasonal phenomena." I have covered the natural world for seven years. Trees that have stood for four millennia do not uproot themselves for seasonal reasons. When the oldest living witnesses to Ardia's history choose flight over remaining in soil they have anchored for longer than human memory extends, we should perhaps consider what they are running from — and whether we will have the option to follow.
