Whisper-of-Morning-Rain Offers Grand Council Her Final Gift: The Names of Every Tree Lost to Silence

Fading forest spirit begins dictating four thousand years of memory to Chronicle scribes as refugee crisis enters second week.

VM
By Vael Moonstrike Environment & Nature Correspondent
24 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

THORNHAVEN — She arrived at the boundary stones as the weakest of the seventeen, her light so dim that the junior wardens mistook her for morning mist. Now Whisper-of-Morning-Rain has become the most important witness to what the Greyward Anomaly is truly destroying — not merely trees and ley-lines, but the accumulated memory of a forest that has stood since before humans learned to write. She is dying, if that word applies to beings who exist as light and recollection, and she has chosen to spend her final days giving us what she carried.

"Every tree has a name," she told me yesterday, her form flickering with each word. "Every stream, every stone where deer drank at dawn, every hollow where owls raised their young for a hundred generations. When the Silence takes us, those names vanish. Not forgotten — unmade. As if they never existed." She has asked the Chronicle to send scribes. She wishes to dictate what she can before her light extinguishes entirely, and I have arranged for three of our archival staff to travel from Praxis with spell-preserved parchment that should hold the weight of what she carries.

The Grand Council's Land and Resources Committee has finally responded to my urgent inquiries — not with policy guidance, but with a formal request that the Chronicle share whatever records we compile. Councillor Ashby of the Reform Coalition called Whisper-of-Morning-Rain's testimony "potentially the most significant environmental documentation in Ardian history," though she acknowledged that the Council has no framework for compensating an incorporeal being for intellectual contribution, nor any precedent for treating spirit memory as legal evidence. The Arcane Academy's Department of Incorporeal Studies has dispatched two researchers to observe the dictation process, though I have insisted they maintain appropriate distance from a being who did not survive four millennia to become a specimen.

Meanwhile, the refugee crisis at Thornhaven's gates continues to worsen. Twenty-three spirits have now gathered at the boundary stones, with reports of dozens more moving slowly through the forest's edge — their passage visible as faint trails of dimming light between the trees. Mayor Ashdale has authorized temporary sanctuary within the city's oldest grove, a stand of heritage oaks planted during the original Treaty negotiations, but the spirits report that proximity to old growth only slows their fading rather than reversing it. Whatever sustains them requires connection to their native groves, and those groves are being consumed by what they call the Silence.

I asked Whisper-of-Morning-Rain what she wanted most recorded, expecting her to name the great Mother-Trees now walking south, or the ancient spirits of deeper power who have not yet fled. Instead, she spoke of a meadow stream where children from Thornhaven once came to skip stones, and a particular boulder where a young half-elven girl sat each summer reading books she borrowed from the border library. "The great things will be remembered," she said, her light steadying briefly with something I recognized as conviction. "It is the small beauties that vanish first. Those are what I will not let the Silence have." The scribes arrive tomorrow. I will ensure every word finds its way to parchment, and from there to the Chronicle's archives, and from there to whatever future we are still capable of building.
