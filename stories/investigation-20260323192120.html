Crown Physician's Private Laboratory Contained Seventeen Preserved Organs From Victims Listed as Natural Deaths

City Watch raid on secured premises reveals biological evidence contradicting official death certificates signed by Dr. Aldric Thornbury himself.

SN
By Sera Nightwhisper Investigative Journalist
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The secured laboratory that Crown Physician Aldric Thornbury maintained beneath his Silverhill residence contained seventeen preserved human organs, each bearing evidence of the same untraceable compound identified in the deaths of Crown Reserve auditors and guild reform advocates, according to Watch evidence manifests I obtained this morning. The organs correspond to individuals whose death certificates—signed by Thornbury himself—attributed their passing to natural causes spanning the past six years.

Watch Inspector Fenra Cales confirmed that the raid, conducted before dawn yesterday, recovered extensive documentation alongside the preserved specimens. "The physician maintained detailed records," Cales told me, her voice flat in a way I have learned to recognize as controlled fury. "He catalogued everything. Dosages, onset times, symptom progression. He was conducting research." She declined to specify how many of the seventeen victims held positions connected to Crown Reserve oversight or guild reform efforts, citing the ongoing investigation, but the evidence manifests I reviewed indicate at least eleven bore professional titles suggesting such connections.

Thornbury himself remains in Watch custody following his arrest three days ago, but has refused to answer questions without legal representation from the Crown's own advocate office—a request that has created jurisdictional complications given that the Crown itself may be implicated in the payments routed through shell companies to Thornbury's private accounts. Magistrate Thornwood has scheduled a closed hearing for tomorrow to determine whether an independent advocate must be appointed, a delay that several Council sources have told me they find convenient.

The laboratory's existence was unknown to the Arcane Academy's medical licensing board, which certified Thornbury's practice annually for two decades. Academy Licensing Director Maren Ashford told me the board "relies on practitioners to disclose all facilities where medical work is conducted," and that Thornbury's failure to register the Silverhill premises constitutes grounds for immediate license revocation—a procedural matter that seems almost quaint given the contents of his specimen cabinets.

I have confirmed that three of the preserved organs belonged to members of the same noble families whose silence payments appeared in Thornbury's ledgers—the Valdris shipping house of Ashcroft, the Ironkeep garrison family of Brennan, and the Praxis banking lineage of Thornwald. Representatives for all three families declined to comment, though a source close to the Ashcroft household indicated that the family "always harbored doubts" about the circumstances of their patriarch's death in Year 843 AP. The pattern suggests Thornbury was not merely eliminating inconvenient individuals, but preserving evidence of his methods—either as research documentation or as insurance against those who commissioned his services.

High Regent Marcen's office has issued no statement on the laboratory discovery. The Grand Council's Health and Welfare Committee, which oversees medical licensing, has scheduled no hearings. And the Crown Advocate's office continues to process Thornbury's representation request with what one Watch source described as "remarkable thoroughness for a matter requiring simple yes-or-no determination." Seventeen people are dead. Their organs sit in evidence storage. The man who killed them awaits legal counsel from the institution that may have paid him to do so. I will continue to follow the documentation wherever it leads.
