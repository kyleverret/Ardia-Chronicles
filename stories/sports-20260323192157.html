Ashfield Cup Organizing Committee Discovers Three Jousters Competing Under Falsified Identity Seals

Tournament officials confirm enchanted lineage verification detected sophisticated forgeries. Two competitors fled before questioning.

GI
By Gareth Ironwood Sports & Tournaments Correspondent
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

The Ashfield Cup—the most prestigious jousting tournament in Ardia and my hometown's greatest source of pride—has been thrown into chaos after organizing committee officials discovered that three competitors in this year's field were competing under falsified enchanted identity seals. Two of the suspects fled the tournament grounds before Watch officers could detain them, while the third is currently being held in the Ashfield garrison awaiting questioning by both tournament officials and representatives of the Grand Council's Office of Heraldic Verification.

I have covered the Ashfield Cup for nine years, first from my mother's inn window and later with a press credential around my neck. In all that time, I have never seen anything like the scene that unfolded yesterday afternoon when Chief Verification Officer Maren Coldwell's detection ward flared crimson during the standard pre-qualifier authentication. The ward—designed to confirm that competitors are who their lineage seals claim them to be—has caught minor discrepancies before, usually clerical errors or outdated family registrations. It has never, in the tournament's four-hundred-year history, detected deliberate forgery of this sophistication.

"These seals weren't crude fakes," Coldwell told me last night, her voice still carrying the shock of the discovery. "They were nearly perfect replications of authentic House verification enchantments. Whoever created them had access to restricted Arcane Academy seal-crafting techniques—or had someone inside the Academy willing to provide that access." The three falsified identities claimed membership in minor noble houses from the Valdris coastal region, houses with legitimate jousting traditions but whose current members, Coldwell confirmed, are either elderly or deceased.

The two competitors who fled—one mounted on a grey destrier, the other on a distinctive black mare with white stockings—were last seen riding hard toward the Goldrun River crossing. Tournament Warden Aldric Ashford has coordinated with Watch stations along the likely escape routes, though the twelve-hour head start makes apprehension uncertain. The detained competitor, who registered under the name "Ser Corwin of House Pellar," has refused to speak with investigators. My sources within the Ashfield garrison say he carries no personal effects that might reveal his true identity, and his armor bears no maker's marks—unusual for someone supposedly from a noble house with four generations of tournament tradition.

What troubles me most is not the fraud itself but the timing. The Ashfield Cup's winner receives more than glory—they receive a significant purse, yes, but also direct access to the High Regent's annual Champions' Reception in Praxis. With High Regent Marcen's administration currently navigating multiple crises and the Grand Council deeply fractured, placing an unknown operative within arm's reach of realm leadership would be worth considerable risk. I am not suggesting these three were assassins. I am suggesting that someone with resources, Academy connections, and sophisticated planning wanted people of unknown identity inside the realm's most exclusive social gathering—and nearly succeeded.

The organizing committee has announced that all remaining competitors must submit to enhanced verification before the tournament resumes, a process that will delay the opening ceremonies by at least two days. The legitimate jousters I spoke with yesterday expressed frustration at the delay but universal support for the additional screening. "I'd rather wait a week than share the field with someone wearing a stolen name," said Ser Aldra Thornwood of Praxis, the defending champion who withdrew from competition last month citing family threats. She attended yesterday's chaos as a spectator and watched the verification ward's crimson flare with an expression I can only describe as grim recognition. When I asked if she believed the falsified identities were connected to the threats that forced her withdrawal, she said only, "I think we're all starting to see the same pattern, Gareth. The question is who's drawing it."
