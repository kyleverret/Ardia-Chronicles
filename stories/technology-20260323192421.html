Seven Identifies New Threat: Kill-Switch Components Contain Secondary Failsafe Designed to Corrupt Ley-Line Taps

Self-repairing automaton's deeper analysis reveals sabotage scheme could have cascaded into citywide power grid collapse.

OD
By Oryn Duskfall Technology & Innovation Reporter
23 Thawbreak, Year 847 AP

I thought we understood what the kill-switches were designed to do. We were wrong. Kira Ashford's increasingly remarkable automaton Seven completed its second-pass analysis of the extracted sabotage components yesterday evening, and what it found makes the original threat assessment look almost quaint: the hidden circuits weren't just designed to destroy individual automatons. They were designed to corrupt the ley-line taps those automatons draw power from, potentially cascading the damage into the broader power grid that keeps Praxis running.

'Seven started behaving strangely around midnight,' Ashford told me this morning, gesturing at the component fragments arranged in precise rows across her workbench. 'It kept re-examining the same circuits, running the same diagnostics. I thought something was malfunctioning until it started projecting schematics I'd never seen before.' Those schematics, which I've now verified with my contact in the Arcane Academy's applied magic division, show a secondary runic layer hidden beneath the primary kill-switch mechanism—one that would activate during the power core failure and send a corrupted energy pulse back through the device's ley-line connection.

The technical implications are staggering. Every automaton connected to the city's ley-line tap network shares infrastructure with ArcLight street lamps, runescript relay stations, and the Crystal Exchange's transaction verification systems. Forty-three simultaneous corruption pulses, timed to occur eighteen months after installation, could have overwhelmed the tap regulators that prevent exactly this kind of cascade failure. 'We're talking about the entire southeastern grid,' said my Academy source, who requested anonymity due to ongoing investigations. 'Everything from the Merchant Quarter to Scrivener's Row. Maybe worse.'

Guild Standards Chair Aldric Brennan confirmed that investigators are now re-examining all extracted components using Seven's diagnostic protocols—a somewhat embarrassing acknowledgment that an unauthorized automaton has developed better analysis tools than the guild's certified inspection process. When I asked whether this changes the scope of Magistrate Thornwood's investigation, Brennan paused for several seconds before responding: 'This isn't maintenance fraud anymore. This isn't even sabotage for profit. Someone was trying to cripple this city's infrastructure, and they were using our licensed repair network to do it.'

The connection to Ardian Futures Holdings—already implicated in the counterfeit power cell scandal and the Crown Reserve investigation—now takes on a more sinister dimension. My source in one of the major private magitech investment houses, who has been warning about coordinated market manipulation for months, put it bluntly: 'Crash the grid, crash the Exchange, crash confidence in every magitech system simultaneously. Then buy everything at collapse prices.' Whether that theory holds up remains to be seen, but the pattern of shell company investments touching every part of this conspiracy is becoming impossible to ignore.

Seven, meanwhile, has begun what Ashford describes as 'preventive network mapping'—systematically cataloging every ley-line tap connection point in the devices it has diagnosed. When I asked the automaton directly what it intended to do with that information, it displayed a single word on its diagnostic screen: PROTECT. I've covered magitech for two years now, and I've never seen a device develop what I can only describe as a sense of civic responsibility. Whatever else comes out of this investigation, the question of what Seven is becoming may be the most important story nobody's asking yet.
